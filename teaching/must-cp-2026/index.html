Course Description

The world exhibits enormous variation in how political life is organized. Some countries are stable democracies with capable states and broad-based prosperity; others face authoritarian rule, extractive institutions, ethnic conflict, or state failure. In this course, we explore explanations for this variation, and some of the institutional responses meant to improve human welfare. Readings draw from political science and related fields, and offer diverse and sometimes violently opposing perspectives. Students will emerge with a deeper understanding of how political life is organized and foundational analytic frameworks for understanding politics across contexts.

Part I lays the foundations of comparative political science: the state, institutions, state capacity, and the collective action problems that pervade political life. Part II turns to political regimes and regime change: how we define democracy and authoritarianism, why countries democratize, and how ethnicity and nationalism shape political competition. Part III applies these frameworks to some of the thorniest questions: institutional design, ethnic conflict, federalism, and political violence.

This is a discussion-based course. The central activity is critical engagement with arguments: identifying what an author claims, evaluating the evidence, and asking whether the argument holds. The course assumes familiarity with fundamental social science concepts, basic undergraduate mathematics, and the ability to engage with academic literature. Experience with political science is not required.

Course Format

This class combines lectures and discussion. Your full participation in class discussions is essential. Complete the readings and come to class ready to participate, ask questions, debate with your colleagues, and contribute to our collective enterprise. Attendance is mandatory.

There is no required textbook. All readings will be distributed as PDFs. The reading load averages roughly 40–55 pages per session. Reading assignments will be sent by email each Friday by 9:00 AM Myanmar time for the following week's sessions. The session topics below are fixed; the specific readings may change week to week.

Some readings are harder than others. Where a reading is particularly dense, the instructor will flag it and indicate what to focus on. Do not be discouraged if you cannot follow every detail. Come to class with the main argument and specific questions, and we will work through the material together.

Participation (40%)

Participation means demonstrating careful reading and the ability to engage with ideas: asking questions, offering interpretations, challenging arguments, and connecting readings to cases. Either asking or answering questions counts. Consistent, informed engagement across all sessions is expected.

Weekly Critical Reading Responses (30%)

Before each session, submit a short written response to the week's readings (300 words maximum). These should not summarize the readings. Instead: identify one argument from the readings, state whether you find it convincing, and explain why or why not. Responses are due before class.

You will have 10 opportunities to submit reading responses. The lowest score will be dropped, so only 9 contribute to your grade.

Part I: Concept Foundations

Session 1: The State, Institutions, and Why Comparison Matters

We introduce the field of comparative politics, the concept of the state, and the central question of why some countries are rich and others poor. We focus on how states became capable historically and what types of institutions lead to prosperity.

  • What is comparative politics and why do we compare?
  • The bellicist theory of state formation: "war made the state, and the state made war"
  • Inclusive vs. extractive institutions as the key driver of prosperity and poverty

O'Neil, Patrick. Essentials of Comparative Politics, Ch. 1: "Introduction to Comparative Politics."
~13 pp. Accessible. Overview of the field: what comparative politics is and why comparison matters.

Tilly, Charles. Coercion, Capital, and European States, AD 990–1992, Ch. 1: "Cities and States in World History," pp. 1–16.
~16 pp. Moderate. The "war made the state" argument — how interstate warfare drove European state formation.

Acemoglu, Daron and James Robinson. Why Nations Fail, Ch. 3: "The Making of Prosperity and Poverty," pp. 70–95.
~25 pp. Accessible. Why some countries are rich and others poor: inclusive vs. extractive institutions.

Session 2: Institutions, Development, and How States See

What does it mean to "develop" a strong state? We explore a genuinely unsolved tension between the need to develop state capacity and the dangers and pitfalls of human schemes to do this.

  • The imperative of state-building: without capacity, neither democracy nor markets function
  • Legibility, high modernism, and the dangers of state simplification
  • Isomorphic mimicry: states that adopt the appearance of capacity without the substance

Fukuyama, Francis. 2004. "The Imperative of State-Building." Journal of Democracy 15(2): 17–24.
~8 pp. Accessible. The case that state capacity is a precondition for both democracy and development.

Scott, James C. 1998. Seeing Like a State (shortened course excerpt from Introduction and Chapter 1).
~17 pp. Moderate. How states create simplified maps of complex societies to govern them, and how ruling through those abstractions can produce policy failure and, at the limit, state failure.

Pritchett, Lant, Michael Woolcock, and Matt Andrews. 2013. "Looking Like a State: Techniques of Persistent Failure in State Capability for Implementation" (shortened course excerpt).
~14 pp. Moderate. "Isomorphic mimicry": why states adopt the appearance of capacity without the substance.

Session 3: Collective Action and Public Goods

Why do people free ride, and when do they cooperate instead? This session introduces collective action as a core problem in comparative politics, from social movements to public goods and shared resources.

  • Free-riding and the collective action problem
  • Public-spirited collective action
  • Public goods, stationary bandits, and the tragedy of the commons

Chong, Dennis. 1991. Collective Action and the Civil Rights Movement, Ch. 1: "Public-Spirited Collective Action."
~8 pp. Accessible. Collective Action Problems: why do people join risky movements instead of free-riding on benefits won by others?

Olson, Mancur. 1993. "Dictatorship, Democracy, and Development." American Political Science Review 87(3): 567–576.
~10 pp. Moderate. Why do rulers who extract from society still provide public goods?

Ostrom, Elinor. 2008. "Tragedy of the Commons." In The New Palgrave Dictionary of Economics.
~5 pp. Accessible. What is the "tragedy of the commons," and how can groups avoid overusing shared resources?

Part II: Regimes and Regime Change

Session 4: Democracy and Authoritarianism

Democracy and authoritarianism are viewed as intuitive concepts. However, defining and operationalizing these terms is a major challenge. This session introduces major frameworks for defining and classifying political regimes.

  • Polyarchy: democracy as participation plus contestation
  • What democracy is and is not (classification challenges)
  • Types of authoritarianism: military, single-party, personalist

Schmitter, Philippe C., and Terry Lynn Karl. 1991. "What Democracy Is . . . and Is Not."
~14 pp. Accessible. What counts as democracy, and why is it harder to define than it first appears?

Diamond, Larry. 2002. "Thinking About Hybrid Regimes."
~9 pp. Accessible. What should we call regimes that hold elections but fall short of democracy?

Frantz, Erica. 2018. Authoritarianism: What Everyone Needs to Know, Chapters 1 and 5 (partial).
~14 pp. Accessible. What is authoritarianism, and how do different authoritarian regime types actually work?

Session 5: Democratization Dynamics

What factors explain why democracy emerges in some countries but not in others? Comparative political science has considered a huge array of explanations. In this session we touch on only a few highly influential and contested theories: modernization and economic development; cultures or religions which are compatible with democracy; and the 'resource curse'.

  • Modernization theory, capitalisim, and democratization
  • Culture, institutions, and political agency in democratic change
  • The resource curse, Islam, and democratization

Huber, Evelyne, Dietrich Rueschemeyer, and John D. Stephens. 1993. "Capitalist Development and Democracy" (course excerpt, pp. 71–78).
~8 pp. Moderate. What social forces generated durable democratic openings?

Kim Dae-jung. 1994. "Is Culture Destiny? The Myth of Asia's Anti-Democratic Values."
~6 pp. Accessible. Are "cultural" explanations persuasive, or do institutions and political choices matter more?

Ross, Michael L. 2011 (course excerpt; in combined packet); Stepan, Alfred, and Graeme B. Robertson. 2003 (course excerpt; in combined packet).
~15 pp. Moderate. What is the 'resource curse' and how has it shaped perceptions of the Muslim World?

Session 6: Ethnicity and Nationalism

What are the effects of diversity on state development? How can we understand the distinction between a state, a nation, and a nation-state? How is identity constructed, and is conflict inevitable in diverse societies? The implications of this session are fundamental for Myanmar.

  • Nations as imagined communities
  • Ethnic identity as constructed
  • Why ethnic diversity undermines public goods provision

Anderson, Benedict. 1983. Imagined Communities: Reflections on the Origin and Spread of Nationalism, Chapters 1 and 10.
26 pp. Moderate. Nations as imagined communities, built through state categories and institutions.

Chandra, Kanchan. 2006. "What Is Ethnic Identity and Does It Matter?"
18 pp. Moderate. What is ethnic identity, and how is it constructed?

Habyarimana, James, Macartan Humphreys, Daniel N. Posner, and Jeremy M. Weinstein. 2007. "Why Does Ethnic Diversity Undermine Public Goods Provision?"
6 pp. Moderate. If ethnicity is constructed or imagined, why is it still useful for producing public goods? This experiment looks at the mechanisms.

Part III: Institutions and Conflict

Session 7: Institutional Design

Political institutions allocate power, structure competition, and shape representation. Majoritarian and power-sharing models offer different answers to the problems of inclusion, accountability, and coalition-building.

  • Majoritarian vs. consensus democracy
  • Electoral systems and their consequences; Duverger's Law
  • Descriptive vs. substantive representation

Horowitz, Donald L. 2003. "Electoral Systems: A Primer for Decision Makers."
13 pp. Accessible. How should we judge an electoral system: by proportionality, accountability, durability, interethnic conciliation, minority inclusion, or something else?

Jung, Courtney, and Ian Shapiro. 1995. "South Africa's Negotiated Transition: Democracy, Opposition, and the New Constitutional Order."
~15 pp. Moderate. Did South Africa's negotiated settlement create democracy, or did it entrench elite power-sharing at the expense of a meaningful opposition?

Koelble, Thomas, and Andrew Reynolds. 1996. "Power-Sharing Democracy in the New South Africa."
~16 pp. Moderate. A rebuttal to Jung and Shapiro: can consensus-oriented power-sharing stabilize democracy in a divided society rather than undermine it?

Session 8: Theories of Ethnic Conflict and the Commitment Problem

Violence often grows from fear, insecurity, and credible commitment problems rather than from ancient hatreds alone. Institutions and electoral incentives also shape when elites inflame violence and when they restrain it.

  • The commitment problem: why rational actors who prefer peace may fight
  • Ethnic conflict as rational fear
  • Electoral competition and ethnic violence

Lake, David A., and Donald Rothchild. 1996. "Containing Fear: The Origins and Management of Ethnic Conflict" (course excerpt).
~16 pp. Moderate. Ethnic conflict is driven less by "ancient hatreds" than by fear, weak states, information failures, commitment problems, and security dilemmas.

Walter, Barbara F. 1999. "Designing Transitions from Civil War: Demobilization, Democratization, and Commitments to Peace" (course excerpt).
~19 pp. Moderate. Why do civil war settlements fail so often? Because combatants often cannot credibly commit to peace during disarmament and political transition.

Session 9: Federalism and Pluralism

Federalism is a central but contested response to pluralism. Territorial devolution can reassure minorities and distribute power, but it can also harden group boundaries and destabilize constitutional bargains.

This session is one of the more theoretically demanding sessions in the course. Please plan for slightly more reading time.
  • Coming-together vs. holding-together federalism
  • Federalism as ethnic conflict management — and its risks
  • Self-enforcing constitutions and the coordination problem

Summarization of Weingast, Barry R. 1997. "The Political Foundations of Democracy and the Rule of Law" (American Political Science Review 91, no. 2). (This PDF is an accessible version of the arguments in Weingast's paper - not the original paper.)
22 pp. Accessible. Constitutions do not enforce themselves; constitutional limits endure when citizens coordinate to defend them.

Horowitz, Donald L. 1985. Ethnic Groups in Conflict, Ch. 15: "Structural Techniques to Reduce Ethnic Conflict" (course excerpt).
~19 pp. Moderate. Federalism can either mitigate or intensify ethnic conflict; its effects depend on boundaries, the number of units, and the incentives it creates.

Session 10: Violence, Nonviolence, and Civil War

The final session brings together themes from across the course, focusing on civil war, revolution, and tactics of democratic resistance.

  • State weakness, not ethnic diversity, predicts civil war
  • The local dynamics of violence: civil wars are messier than master narratives suggest
  • Preference falsification and revolutionary surprise
  • How nonviolent resistance and violent resistance differ

Diversity and Inclusion

This course does not tolerate any form of discrimination, harassment, or retaliation. All members of the class are expected to help ensure that others can learn in a supportive and respectful environment.

Email Communication

Students are expected to read email sent from the instructor. Failure to read and react to communications in a timely manner does not absolve the student from knowing and complying with the content of the communications.

Classroom Recording

Students may not record classroom lectures, discussion, or activities without advance written permission of the instructor.

Sensitive Topics

This course engages directly with political violence, ethnic conflict, state repression, and authoritarianism — topics that may be personally significant. The goal is rigorous, evidence-based discussion. Students are encouraged but never obligated to draw on personal experience. The instructor is available to discuss any concerns privately.

Privacy

You are not required to use your real name or use video during class. However, you are expected to speak in class and share ideas. Please discuss any concerns with the instructor.
